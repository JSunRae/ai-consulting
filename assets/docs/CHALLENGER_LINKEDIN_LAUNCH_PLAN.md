# Challenger LinkedIn Launch Plan

Prepared: `2026-05-09`

## What changed on the site

- Homepage now teaches three Challenger-style ideas before selling:
  - AI software diligence
  - customer-service AI reality checks
  - build-vs-buy decision logic
- Services page now includes:
  - public entry pricing for the Diagnostic Review
  - a dedicated AI Software & Vendor Due Diligence offer
  - explicit messaging on support automation and headcount assumptions
- Contact page now invites buyers to describe the decision or workflow, not just the tool.
- Chat and voice intake content now handle software-selection, vendor-risk, service-automation, and build-vs-buy conversations directly.

## Pricing and marketing recommendation

- Show the first paid step publicly.
- Keep the 20-minute fit call free, but frame it as qualification only.
- Publish the Diagnostic Review at `EUR 950 net`.
- Scope larger sprints, vendor diligence projects, implementation work, and retainers only after diagnosis.

Reason:

- Public entry pricing reduces friction.
- It filters out weak leads without commoditizing the higher-value work.
- It supports a Challenger posture because you still control the larger commercial conversation instead of turning the site into a rate card.

## Launch cadence

- Post `2` times per week for the first `5` weeks.
- Use Tuesday and Thursday mornings in `Europe/Berlin`.
- Reply to comments the same day.
- For each post, direct readers to the site only when the post naturally creates buying curiosity.

## Execution rule

- `assets/data/social-posts.json` is the only operational state store for launch-ready, queued, scheduled, published, and imported social posts.
- The launch plan defines the commercial sequence, but scheduling, CTA routing, and publish state must live on the post records themselves.
- Older records may omit launch metadata; scripts and rendering must tolerate that.

## Ranked launch order

### 1. Before you buy AI software, ask what you are actually buying

Why first:

- Strong Challenger reframe
- speaks to real buyer confusion
- creates immediate relevance for software selection work

LinkedIn copy:

Before you buy AI software, ask what you are actually buying.

Not all “AI products” are the same.

Some are:

- fine-tuned for a narrow behavior
- retrieval-based on top of your documents
- orchestration layers around a major model
- thin wrappers around an API call

Those differences matter.

They affect:

- where your data goes
- how permissions are handled
- what happens when the model fails
- what the usage cost looks like at scale
- whether the product creates any durable advantage at all

The demo usually shows the best-case answer.

The buying decision should focus on the operating reality around the answer.

If the vendor cannot explain architecture, failure modes, logging, permissions, and production cost clearly, the product may not be ready for a serious workflow.

### 2. Wanting to reduce customer-service headcount? Start with avoidable contacts

Why second:

- hits a high-interest commercial pain point
- teaches instead of sounding anti-AI
- opens customer-service audit work

LinkedIn copy:

Wanting to reduce customer-service headcount with AI?

Start with a harder question:

Why are customers contacting support in the first place?

If the ticket volume is driven by:

- unclear invoices
- weak onboarding
- poor product documentation
- missing order visibility
- messy escalation rules

then a chatbot may automate the symptom before you fix the cause.

That can lower visible cost on first contact while increasing repeat contacts, frustration, and cleanup work behind the scenes.

The safer first move is often:

1. classify ticket demand
2. identify avoidable contacts
3. improve internal knowledge access
4. design escalation properly
5. then automate the right interactions

A chatbot is not a customer-service strategy.

### 3. Fine-tuned does not automatically mean better

Why third:

- sharpens technical credibility
- helps buyers understand architecture without jargon overload

LinkedIn copy:

“Fine-tuned” does not automatically mean better.

Buyers hear the phrase and assume the product must be more specialized.

Sometimes that is true.

But in many business workflows, the real question is not whether the model was fine-tuned.

It is:

- what information the system can retrieve
- whether that information is current
- whether permissions are respected
- how the system behaves when the answer is unclear

If the challenge is company-specific knowledge, retrieval quality and data hygiene may matter more than fine-tuning.

The model is only one part of the product.

For real commercial use, the surrounding system design usually matters more than the label in the pitch deck.

### 4. AI savings are often theoretical until roles change

Why fourth:

- good CFO-friendly angle
- helps qualify serious transformation buyers

LinkedIn copy:

AI savings are often theoretical until roles change.

A team can save 20 minutes here and 15 minutes there and still produce no real financial benefit.

Why?

Because time savings do not automatically become cost savings.

Unless management decides how the capacity will be captured, AI may create convenience without changing the operating model.

That is why I ask three questions early:

1. Which tasks disappear?
2. Which new review tasks appear?
3. Who owns the benefit after the workflow changes?

If nobody can answer those clearly, the ROI case is probably still optimistic rather than commercial.

### 5. The cheapest AI tool can be the most expensive one

Why fifth:

- strong procurement hook
- easy post for executives and operators to engage with

LinkedIn copy:

The cheapest AI tool can be the most expensive one.

Licence cost is only part of the picture.

The real cost is often hidden in:

- human review time
- escalation cleanup
- integration work
- exception handling
- compliance controls
- poor answers that sound confident

That is why cost per licence is a weak buying metric.

The better question is:

What is the cost per trusted output?

A cheaper tool that creates constant checking, rework, and risk can destroy the business case quietly.

### 6. If the AI cannot help your own team first, do not put it in front of customers

Why sixth:

- practical service-design point
- lowers resistance because it offers a safer first step

LinkedIn copy:

If the AI cannot help your own trained team first, do not put it in front of customers.

That sounds strict, but it is useful discipline.

An internal copilot lets you learn:

- where the knowledge base is weak
- where escalation rules fail
- which prompts create risk
- where staff still need evidence or traceability

That is a much safer learning environment than a public-facing bot.

Customer-facing AI should come after the business understands where the system is helpful, where it is fragile, and where a human needs to step in.

### 7. The model name tells you less than you think

Why seventh:

- simple post with strong shareability
- supports architecture positioning

LinkedIn copy:

The model name tells you less than you think.

Two vendors can use the same underlying model and produce very different outcomes.

Why?

Because enterprise value usually sits in the surrounding system:

- retrieval quality
- permissions
- logging
- UX
- validation
- escalation
- workflow fit

Buyers often anchor on “it uses GPT” or “it uses Claude.”

That is too shallow.

The model matters.

But the system around the model is where most of the operational value or risk actually lives.

### 8. Do not start with a chatbot. Start with the decision

Why eighth:

- clear Challenger line
- broad enough to reinforce your overall positioning

LinkedIn copy:

Do not start with a chatbot.

Start with the decision you want to improve.

That might be:

- faster support resolution
- cleaner pricing exceptions
- fewer forecast surprises
- better account prioritization
- faster executive reporting

Once the decision is clear, the rest becomes much easier to judge:

- what data is needed
- what level of AI is appropriate
- what should stay human
- what good performance looks like

Tool-first projects drift because success was never defined properly.

Decision-first projects usually find the right level of automation much faster.

### 9. Review burden kills more AI ROI than most teams admit

Why ninth:

- very practical
- useful for both technical and non-technical buyers

LinkedIn copy:

Review burden kills more AI ROI than most teams admit.

When an AI system produces a result that still needs heavy checking, the business has not removed work.

It has changed the shape of work.

That is not always bad.

But it needs to be measured honestly.

If every output still requires:

- fact checking
- approval
- source tracing
- exception handling

then the labour case may be much weaker than the demo suggests.

Good AI design is not only about generating outputs.

It is about reducing trusted effort.

### 10. A good AI vendor should explain how the system fails

Why tenth:

- strong closing post for the first launch wave
- turns curiosity into a clear due-diligence conversation

LinkedIn copy:

A good AI vendor should explain how the system fails.

The demo shows success cases.

The buying conversation should test failure cases.

Ask:

- when does the system abstain?
- when does it escalate?
- what happens when data is missing?
- how are permissions enforced?
- what gets logged?
- how do we audit a bad answer later?

If the vendor cannot answer those clearly, the product may not be ready for a critical business workflow.

The most dangerous AI output is not the one that crashes.

It is the one that sounds right, gets trusted, and quietly drives the wrong action.

## Launch metadata contract

Each launch-ready archive record should carry these fields where relevant:

- `launchWave`: named execution batch such as `linkedin-launch-q2-2026`
- `launchPriority`: ranked publish order within the wave
- `pillar`: commercial theme or offer angle
- `status`: current operational state in the archive
- `contentType`: evergreen, news, case-study, historical, or directional
- `sourceBlogUrl`: source article or source page
- `targetPageUrl`: concrete destination URL for the CTA
- `ctaType`: `book-fit-call`, `start-diagnostic-review`, or `download-checklist`
- `preparedDate`: date the post became launch-ready
- `commercialIntent`: lead-magnet, vendor-diligence, diagnostic, workflow-advisory, or authority-builder
- `offerAlignment`: related service or lead magnet
- `schedule`: suggested publish timing and route
