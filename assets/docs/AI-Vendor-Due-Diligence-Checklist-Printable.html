Buyer Aid

AI Vendor Due Diligence Checklist

Use this before you approve a pilot, sign a contract, or let an AI tool into a live workflow. The goal is not to admire the demo. The goal is to understand what you are actually buying, where the risk sits, and whether the workflow can absorb it.

Best for

Leadership, finance, BI, procurement, and operations teams evaluating AI software.

Use when

A vendor pitch is moving faster than the commercial, security, or workflow questions.

Prepared by

Jason Rae
Commercial Analytics & Applied AI Leader

How to use this checklist

Use the sections below in architecture review, procurement, finance sign-off, or pilot-governance meetings. If key answers remain vague, the buying process is probably moving too early.

1. Commercial fit

  • What business decision or workflow should improve?
  • What problem makes this worth solving now?
  • Which business metric should move if it works?
  • Who owns the result after go-live?

2. Product architecture

  • Is it fine-tuned, retrieval-based, prompt-orchestrated, or mostly a wrapper?
  • Which model providers sit underneath the product?
  • What proprietary logic belongs to the vendor?
  • What happens when the model is uncertain or wrong?

3. Data flow and security

  • Where does user input go?
  • Is company data sent to a third-party provider?
  • Are prompts or outputs logged or stored?
  • Is any layer using your data for training?
  • Which subprocessors are involved?
  • Where is the data hosted geographically?

4. Permissions and governance

  • Does the system respect document-level and role-level permissions?
  • Can it retrieve information users should not see?
  • Can risky actions be blocked, escalated, or reversed?
  • Does it support auditability after an incident?

5. Output reliability

  • Does the system cite sources when it answers from company knowledge?
  • Are the citations actually grounded?
  • What are the known failure modes?
  • What abstention or escalation mechanisms exist?
  • How much human review still remains per trusted output?

6. Workflow fit

  • Is the workflow stable enough to automate safely?
  • What exception rate exists today?
  • Will the tool remove work or shift work into cleanup?
  • What approvals, handoffs, and ownership changes are required?
  • Which systems must it integrate with?

7. Cost and scalability

  • What is the license cost?
  • What usage-based costs sit underneath it?
  • What happens at real production volume?
  • Have retries, long context windows, and heavy document loads been modeled?
  • What support and monitoring burden remains internally?

8. Build vs buy

  • Is the workflow generic enough for off-the-shelf software?
  • Is tighter internal control safer than a vendor layer?
  • Would a lean API workflow solve the problem more cleanly?
  • What is the switching cost if the tool underperforms?

9. Go / no-go gate

Proceed with controls
Run a contained pilot
Re-scope before buying
Reject
Final test:

If the vendor cannot explain what the system really is, where your data goes, how it fails, what it costs at scale, and why the workflow can absorb it, you are probably not ready to buy.
